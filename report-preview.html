Strategic Diagnostic Assessment
The constraint at BW Advisory Solutions is not the Auror platform — it is middle management's failure to connect daily operational decisions to the strategic imperative Brad has set.
Prepared for: Brad Warburton, CEO, BW Advisory Solutions
Date: 18 April 2026
1
Strategic Reality Assessment
Brad has defined a clear and binary success condition — full Auror adoption coupled with measurable reductions in violence and retail crime — yet rates strategy-to-behaviour translation at 3 out of 5, signalling that the executive intent is not landing where it matters.
Middle management has been identified as both the capability gap and the primary constraint, and prior interventions — workshops, strategy sessions, and incentives — have failed to shift this, suggesting the problem is structural rather than motivational.
Decision-making authorities and real-time frontline visibility are both rated at 3, pointing to an accountability architecture that is ambiguous enough to let middle managers deprioritise Auror adoption without consequence.
2
Core Constraint Analysis
Primary Constraint
Middle management does not understand what Auror adoption demands of them operationally, nor why it is non-negotiable. Until that changes, the platform will remain underutilised and the crime reduction objective will stall regardless of executive intent.
Contributing Factors
Systems and processes are not yet configured to make Auror the path of least resistance for frontline teams, meaning adoption competes with existing habits rather than replacing them.
Strategic clarity at the middle management tier is weak — without a shared, operational definition of what full adoption looks like and how it connects to safety outcomes, each manager interprets the mandate differently.
Root Cause Hypothesis
The root cause is likely a translation failure between executive strategy and operational expectation. Brad has set the destination, but middle managers have not been given a precise, role-specific account of what they must do differently each week to get there. Previous workshops and incentives addressed awareness and motivation but left accountability and process design untouched — so behaviour reverted. Without clear decision rights, defined adoption milestones, and visible performance data flowing to leadership in real time, middle managers face no meaningful consequence for inaction, and the strategic objective remains aspirational rather than operational.
3
Recommended Path Forward
1
Define, in writing, what full Auror adoption means at the team level — specific behaviours, usage thresholds, and reporting cadences — then cascade this as a non-negotiable operational standard through every middle manager, with Brad's direct authority behind it.
2
Establish a fortnightly adoption dashboard that surfaces individual team performance against those defined standards, and hold middle managers accountable in one-on-one reviews — removing ambiguity about whether this is being measured and by whom.
Closing the gap between strategic intent and frontline behaviour in an environment where prior interventions have already fallen short requires more than another communication push — it requires a structured look at accountability design, process architecture, and the signals middle managers are actually responding to. A focused diagnostic engagement would surface precisely where the mechanism is breaking down and establish the sequenced steps needed to build durable adoption. Brad is asking the right question; the work now is to answer it with the specificity the situation demands.
